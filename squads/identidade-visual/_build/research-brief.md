# Research Brief: Branding & Visual Identity for Pediatric Healthcare Clinics
**Prepared for:** Identidade Visual Squad — Clínica Base  
**Date:** 2026-05-04  
**Purpose:** Feed the design of an AI squad that creates brand identity (logo, colors, typography, branding kit) for a pediatric occupational therapy clinic using the B.A.S.E. Method.

---

## Area 1: Brand Archetypes for Healthcare

### Most Relevant Frameworks

#### Framework 1: Carl Jung's 12 Brand Archetypes (as applied to healthcare)
The most widely used framework in brand strategy. For healthcare, two archetypes dominate:

- **The Caregiver** — Defined by empathy, compassion, nurturing, reliability, self-sacrifice, and protection. This is the default-fit archetype for most healthcare organizations, especially those providing direct patient care (clinics, pediatrics, therapy). Real-world example: Johnson & Johnson ("The Family Company"), Pampers, Band-Aid. The Caregiver excels when the brand needs to communicate safety, warmth, and personal connection.
- **The Sage** — Defined by knowledge, expertise, truth-seeking, wisdom, and evidence. Best fit for teaching hospitals, life sciences, research-heavy institutions. Risk: can feel cold or distant when the target audience needs emotional connection. Real-world example: Mayo Clinic, WebMD.

**For pediatric occupational therapy (like Clínica Base):** The Caregiver is the primary archetype. The Sage works well as a secondary archetype to signal expertise and clinical credibility without sacrificing warmth.

#### Framework 2: Dominant + Secondary Archetype Blending (Perficient / Kaye Putnam)
Most strong healthcare brands select one dominant archetype and one secondary archetype to avoid a one-dimensional brand voice. The combination creates texture:

| Primary | Secondary | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Caregiver | Sage | Warm + Expert: "We care AND we know what we're doing" |
| Caregiver | Innocent | Warm + Pure: "We believe in a better world for your child" |
| Sage | Caregiver | Expert + Compassionate: used by academic medical centers |

**Recommended for Clínica Base:** Caregiver (primary) + Sage (secondary). The mother is the protagonist of treatment (Caregiver), and the B.A.S.E. Method signals methodological expertise (Sage).

### Actionable Recommendations
1. Build all brand messaging around the Caregiver's core promise: "You are not alone in this."
2. Use Sage elements to communicate the B.A.S.E. Method as a structured, evidence-informed approach — not just intuition.
3. Avoid the Hero archetype (too aggressive/competitive) and the Ruler archetype (too hierarchical for a parent-centered model).
4. Caregiver visual language: rounded shapes, handmade/organic elements, warm and soft tones, images centered on connection and touch.
5. Sage visual language: clean structure, clear typography hierarchy, use of data/method naming, professional credentialing visible (CREFITO).

### Professional Terms Used in Brand Archetype Design
- Brand archetype, archetypal brand strategy, primary/secondary archetype
- Brand personality, brand voice, brand values
- Emotional positioning, consumer-brand relationship
- Archetype expression, visual archetype signals

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Trying to embody 3+ archetypes simultaneously — creates incoherence
- Choosing Sage as primary for a patient-facing pediatric brand — risks emotional distance
- Letting the Caregiver become "martyr-like" — avoid visuals of sacrifice/exhaustion; focus on empowerment
- Ignoring the archetype when writing copy — visual and verbal identity must match

## Area 2: Color Psychology for Pediatric Healthcare

### Most Relevant Frameworks

#### Framework 1: Clinical Trust Colors vs. Pediatric Approachability Colors
Healthcare branding faces a dual challenge: clinical authority (trust, hygiene, expertise) AND parent/child friendliness (warmth, joy, safety). These have different color pulls:

| Goal | Colors | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Trust + Authority | Blue (especially muted/cool tones) | Stability, security, knowledge — used in ~85% of all healthcare logos |
| Cleanliness + Care | White, Light Gray | Hygiene, clarity, space |
| Approachability + Growth | Green (soft/sage) | Nature, healing, health, growth |
| Warmth + Friendliness | Orange, Warm Yellow | Energy, optimism, connection — used by CVS Health, Cigna |
| Pediatric Joy | Soft rainbow accents, Teal, Coral | Age-appropriate, comfort, play |

#### Framework 2: Primary Palette + Accent Strategy (for pediatric clinics specifically)
Design professionals recommend a 2–3 color primary palette anchored in trust colors (blue/green) with a secondary palette of pediatric-appropriate accents (warm tones, softer hues). Avoid overwhelming saturation — use soothing bases with playful accents rather than full-brightness primary colors.

**For pediatric urgent care and therapy:** Light greens, soft teal, a touch of warm orange or coral. Avoid red (clinical alarm) and pure bright yellow (overstimulation).

### Specific Color Recommendations for Pediatric Occupational Therapy

1. **Primary anchor — Soft teal or sage green:** Bridges trust (blue-adjacent) and growth/nature (green). Signals healing, calm, and development without the coldness of pure clinical blue.
2. **Warm accent — Soft coral or warm amber:** Provides the parent-friendliness, warmth, and approachability signal. Avoids the aggression of pure red or the visual noise of bright orange.
3. **Neutral base — Warm white or light cream:** More human and less clinical than pure white. Works for backgrounds, forms, printed materials.
4. **Secondary trust — Muted navy or deep teal:** For headers, authority contexts, professional materials targeting referring physicians or partners (like DesenvolvaCentro).
5. **Pediatric accent — Soft lavender or light yellow:** Can be used sparingly in child-facing materials (reports for parents, therapy room visuals) to add playfulness without compromising brand consistency.

### Caregiver Archetype Color Palette (aligned with brand archetype)
Per brand archetype design research: soft blues, calming greens, warm creams, pinks/corals are natural fits for the Caregiver brand. Blue = dependability and peace. Coral/pink = nurturing, open-hearted. Green = growth and safety.

### Professional Terms Used in Color Design
- Color psychology, chromotherapy reference
- Primary palette, secondary palette, accent colors
- HEX (digital), RGB (screen), CMYK (print), Pantone (physical brand)
- Color harmony, complementary colors, analogous palette
- Brand color standards, color usage rules

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Using pure clinical white + cold blue only — reads as "hospital" not "welcoming clinic"
- Oversaturating with bright rainbow colors — works for a clown, not a credible therapy brand
- No warm tones at all — misses the Caregiver's emotional temperature
- More than 3 primary colors — creates visual noise; reserve extra colors for the secondary palette
- Red as a primary color — associated with emergency, danger, and urgency — wrong signal for therapy

## Area 3: Logo Design Principles for Pediatric Healthcare

### Most Relevant Frameworks

#### Framework 1: The 5 Criteria for Effective Healthcare Logos
Sourced from medical logo design guides (Magnt, Wix, Angelique Vestil):
1. **Simple** — Clean, minimal, instantly readable. No clutter.
2. **Memorable** — Distinctive enough to be recalled after one exposure.
3. **Versatile** — Works at any size (app icon → billboard), in color and monochrome.
4. **Timeless** — Avoids trend-chasing. A pediatric therapy clinic logo should work in 10 years.
5. **Appropriate** — Visually calibrated for the specific audience (children + parents, not general adult patients).

#### Framework 2: Symbol Strategy for Pediatric Specialties
Generic medical symbols (cross, caduceus, stethoscope, heartbeat line) create recognition but zero differentiation. For specialty practices like pediatric OT:
- Use **warm, child-appropriate metaphors**: stars, hands, leaves, abstract play shapes, houses, trees, circles
- Integrate the brand's method or unique value into the symbol (e.g., the idea of "base," "foundation," movement, or sensory development)
- **Subtle customization** beats generic: if using a recognizable symbol, transform it — integrate into a letterform, use organic lines, or combine with a child/family silhouette

### Actionable Recommendations
1. Design a **wordmark or lettermark** combined with a simple icon — avoid icon-only logos at this stage of brand awareness.
2. The icon should reference the clinic's differentiation: the B.A.S.E. Method, child development, play, or the mother-child connection — not generic medicine.
3. Use **rounded letterforms** in the logotype — geometric hard-edged fonts read as cold; rounded signals care, openness, and approachability.
4. Test the logo at 16x16px (favicon size) and 10m distance (signage) — if it breaks, it's too complex.
5. Deliver at minimum 5 logo variations: primary (full color), white/reversed, dark/monochrome, horizontal, and stacked (vertical).

### Pediatric Healthcare Logo Symbol Ideas (researched from 99designs, DesignMantic)
- Stylized child figure in motion (play, reach, movement)
- Abstract leaf or sprout (growth, development)
- Interconnected circles (connection, family, method steps)
- Stylized hand holding/supporting a smaller shape (therapy, care)
- House/shelter shape (base, safety, foundation — directly ties to "Base" name)
- Letterform integration: "B" shaped as a figure or embracing form

### Professional Terms Used in Logo Design
- Wordmark, lettermark, pictorial mark, abstract mark, combination mark
- Logo lockup, logo system, logo variations
- Negative space, visual weight, optical balance
- Scalability, versatility, monochrome version
- Primary mark, secondary mark, favicon/app icon version
- Safe area, exclusion zone, minimum size

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Using stock art or clip-art icons — kills credibility and uniqueness
- Too many fonts in the logo (max 2 font families total in entire brand)
- Overly literal symbols (stethoscope + cross = zero differentiation for a therapy clinic)
- Gradients that don't reproduce in single-color contexts (embroidery, stamps, fax headers)
- Trendy design choices that date quickly (neon, excessive shadows, overlapping translucent shapes)
- Designing only for screen — the logo must work on clinic signage, uniforms, printed forms, referral letters

## Area 4: Typography for Professional Healthcare Branding

### Most Relevant Frameworks

#### Framework 1: Font Category Roles in Healthcare (Progress / Healthy Marketing)
| Category | Signal | Best Use |
|---|---|---|
| Humanist Sans-Serif | Warm, readable, modern, approachable | Headings + body for patient-facing brands |
| Geometric Sans-Serif | Systematic, modern, clean | Tech/innovation-forward healthcare |
| Transitional/Old Style Serif | Traditional, trustworthy, established | Academic institutions, teaching hospitals |
| Slab Serif | Confident, approachable, personality | Mid-size clinics wanting character |
| Script/Display | Warmth, personal, human | Accent only — never for body text |

**Recommendation for pediatric OT clinics:** Humanist sans-serif as primary. Optional: pair with a slab serif or transitional serif for headline contrast.

#### Framework 2: Font Pairing Strategy (Open Sans + PT Serif model)
A two-font system (one sans, one serif) creates visual hierarchy and personality:
- **Heading font** — More personality, larger, establishes brand character
- **Body font** — Maximum legibility, neutral, functional

For healthcare: prioritize legibility above all. The "c," "e," "o" test: enough white space inside these letters = legible at all sizes.

### Specific Font Recommendations

**Primary (Heading/Brand):**
- **Nunito** — Rounded terminal humanist sans-serif. Warm, friendly, professional. Ideal for pediatric and wellness brands where approachability is essential. Available on Google Fonts (no cost).
- **Poppins** — Geometric-adjacent with friendly proportions. Modern and systematic. Used widely in health-tech and progressive health brands. Google Fonts.
- **Plus Jakarta Sans** — Contemporary humanist sans. Warm yet refined. Growing use in health/wellness branding. Google Fonts.

**Secondary (Body/Support):**
- **Lato** — Warm and elegant. Humanist. Highly legible in body text. Industry standard in health organizations (Boston Health Care for the Homeless Program uses it).
- **Open Sans** — Neutral, optimized for screen legibility. Pairs well with any heading font.
- **Inter** — Highly legible at small sizes. Good for clinical/data contexts (financial docs, clinical forms).

**Recommended pairing for Clínica Base:**
- Heading: **Nunito** (warm, rounded, pediatric-friendly, professional)
- Body: **Lato** (warm but neutral, maximum legibility)
- Both free on Google Fonts, widely supported across platforms

### Actionable Recommendations
1. Never use more than 2 font families in the brand system.
2. Define 4–5 type styles (H1, H2, H3, body, caption) with specific sizes and weights for each.
3. Avoid decorative or script fonts for anything requiring legibility (forms, reports, small-size applications).
4. Ensure the chosen fonts are available in Portuguese character sets (accented characters: ã, ç, é, ê, etc.).
5. Define a web-safe fallback for each brand font (e.g., Nunito → fallback: system-ui, sans-serif).

### Professional Terms Used in Typography
- Typeface, font family, font weight (Light, Regular, Medium, SemiBold, Bold)
- Type hierarchy, typographic scale
- Kerning, tracking, leading (line-height)
- Readability vs. legibility
- Web font, variable font, Google Fonts, Adobe Fonts
- Type specimen, type system, body copy, display type

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Using more than 2 font families (creates visual chaos)
- Using decorative or script fonts for body text or any text below 14px
- Ignoring Portuguese character support — accented characters that render incorrectly destroy brand credibility
- Choosing a font because it looks good at 72px but ignoring how it reads at 12px (form labels, fine print)
- Not defining font weights — "bold when needed" without a system creates inconsistency

## Area 5: Branding Kit Structure — Complete Components

### Standard Brand Identity Kit Deliverables

A complete brand identity kit includes two layers: **Brand Assets** (the files) and **Brand Guidelines** (how to use them). Both are required for a professional deliverable.

---

### Layer 1: Brand Assets

#### 1. Logo System (minimum 5 variations)
| Variation | Description |
|---|---|
| Primary mark | Full color, standard orientation (horizontal or stacked) |
| Reversed/White | White version for dark backgrounds |
| Dark/Monochrome | Single-color dark version for stamps, embroidery, fax |
| Horizontal lockup | Logo + wordmark side by side |
| Stacked lockup | Logo above wordmark |
| Favicon/App icon | Simplified mark at 16–64px (icon only, no wordmark) |

File formats: SVG (master), PNG (transparent, @1x/@2x/@3x), PDF (print-ready), EPS (professional print)

#### 2. Color Palette
| Set | Contents |
|---|---|
| Primary palette | 2–3 main brand colors with HEX, RGB, CMYK, Pantone codes |
| Secondary palette | 2–4 supporting accent/neutral colors with same codes |
| Color usage rules | Which color for what context (backgrounds, text, CTAs, borders) |
| Accessibility notes | Minimum contrast ratios (WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for normal text) |

#### 3. Typography System
| Item | Contents |
|---|---|
| Primary typeface | Font name, source (Google Fonts / Adobe / licensed), weights used |
| Secondary typeface | Same details |
| Type hierarchy | H1 through body/caption: size, weight, line-height, letter-spacing specs |
| Type specimen | Visual demonstration of all type styles |
| Fallback stack | Web-safe alternatives when brand fonts aren't available |

#### 4. Graphic Elements / Visual Language
- Brand patterns or textures (if any)
- Iconography style and sample icon set
- Illustration style guide (if illustrations are used)
- Photography style guide (mood, composition, color treatment, subject matter)
- Background treatments (gradients, textures, overlays)

#### 5. Brand Voice & Messaging (often included in complete kits)
- Brand positioning statement (what the brand does, for whom, differently)
- Tagline options
- Tone of voice description (formal/informal, warm/clinical, etc.)
- Words to use / words to avoid
- Sample copy in brand voice (social post, headline, about text)

---

### Layer 2: Brand Guidelines Document

A PDF/interactive document (typically 20–60 pages for a clinic) covering:

1. **Brand Introduction** — mission, vision, values, archetype, personality
2. **Logo Usage** — correct uses, minimum sizes, exclusion zone (safe area), do's and don'ts
3. **Color Standards** — palette with codes, usage rules, incorrect color usage examples
4. **Typography Standards** — hierarchy specs, pairing rules, incorrect type usage
5. **Photography & Imagery** — style direction, mood board, sample approved images
6. **Application Examples** — how the brand looks on: business card, letterhead, Instagram post, clinic signage, WhatsApp profile, email signature, uniform/scrub embroidery
7. **Digital Standards** — social media profile sizes, email template specs, website style notes
8. **Print Standards** — bleed, margin, resolution requirements (300dpi minimum)

---

### Practical Application Kit for a Clinic (Clínica Base scope)

| Deliverable | Format | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Logo system (5 variations) | SVG, PNG, PDF | All applications |
| Color palette swatch file | ASE (Adobe), PNG reference | Design tool use |
| Typography specimen | PDF | Designer reference |
| Brand guidelines | PDF (20–40 pages) | Team + vendors |
| Social media templates | Canva / Figma | Instagram, WhatsApp, LinkedIn |
| Business card design | PDF print-ready | Professional networking |
| Letterhead / referral letter | DOCX + PDF | Clinical correspondence |
| Email signature | HTML | Gmail / Outlook |
| WhatsApp cover / profile | PNG | Messaging channels |
| Clinic signage mockup | PDF | Physical space |

### Professional Terms Used in Brand Kit Design
- Brand guidelines, style guide, brand bible, visual identity standards
- Logo lockup, logo system, primary mark, secondary mark
- Asset library, brand asset management (BAM)
- Safe area / exclusion zone, minimum clear space
- Pantone (PMS), CMYK, RGB, HEX, sRGB
- Brand touchpoints, brand application, mockup
- Master file, working file, production-ready file
- Brand consistency, visual coherence, brand governance

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Delivering only one logo variation (PNG on white background) — unusable for real-world applications
- Not providing CMYK values — digital RGB colors shift in print
- Skipping the "do's and don'ts" section — vendors and team members will misuse the brand without it
- Not specifying minimum logo size — logos placed too small become illegible
- No exclusion zone around logo — other elements crowd the mark
- Forgetting Portuguese/local platform templates (WhatsApp is primary communication in Brazil, not just email)

## Synthesis: Clínica Base Brand Identity Direction

Based on all research, the following positioning emerges as most coherent for Clínica Base:

| Dimension | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Primary Archetype | Caregiver | Pediatric therapy, mother-centered method |
| Secondary Archetype | Sage | B.A.S.E. Method signals structured expertise |
| Primary Color | Soft teal / sage green | Trust + growth + calm; bridges clinical and warm |
| Accent Color | Warm coral / amber | Parent-friendliness, warmth, Caregiver emotional tone |
| Neutral | Warm cream / off-white | Human, not cold clinical white |
| Heading Font | Nunito (rounded, warm, approachable) | Pediatric + professional balance |
| Body Font | Lato (legible, warm, neutral) | Healthcare standard, excellent readability |
| Logo Type | Combination mark (icon + wordmark) | Builds recognition for emerging brand |
| Logo Shape Direction | Rounded, organic, non-generic | Avoid medical clichés; reference B.A.S.E./play/development |
| Tone | Informal but expert | Matches CLAUDE.md brand voice guidelines |
